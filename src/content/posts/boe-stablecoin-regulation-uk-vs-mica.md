# Bank of England Eases Stablecoin Rules: What It Means for Crypto and Why MiCA Still Wins for Token Issuers

The Bank of England has softened its proposed stablecoin regulatory framework, dropping controversial caps on how much sterling-denominated stablecoin users and businesses could hold. On the surface, this sounds like a crypto-friendly pivot from a historically cautious central bank. But the nuance matters enormously — especially if you're a small company deciding where to set up shop and issue tokens.

---

## Part I — UK vs. MiCA: A Direct Comparison

This is the question everyone in the crypto space is asking after the BoE announcement: *has the UK just become a better jurisdiction than EU's MiCA for crypto projects?*

The short answer is: **it depends entirely on what you're building.**

| Criterion | UK | EU (MiCA) |
|---|---|---|
| Market reach after licensing | UK only | 27 EU member states |
| Regulatory clarity today | Low — FCA still finalizing rules | High — in full force from July 1, 2026 |
| Sterling stablecoins | More liberal (no holding caps) | Requires bank/EMI license equivalent |
| Utility / governance tokens | No dedicated framework yet | Covered under MiCA (lighter requirements) |
| Time to license | Unknown — rules still being written | Defined process, established timelines |
| Institutional partner appetite | Uncertain (legal ambiguity) | Growing (clear compliance baseline) |
| Exchange listing acceptance | Mixed | Improving, MiCA compliance increasingly required |

### What the BoE Framework Actually Covers

A critical point that often gets lost in headlines: the new BoE regime applies **exclusively to sterling-denominated systemic stablecoins** — tokens pegged 1:1 to GBP that have reached or are approaching £40 billion in aggregate issuance. That is a very narrow category.

If you are building any of the following, the BoE stablecoin framework is irrelevant to you:
- A utility token for a protocol or dApp
- A governance token for a DAO
- A stablecoin pegged to USD, EUR, or a basket of assets
- An NFT project
- Any DeFi protocol token

The "freedom" the BoE announced is specifically for large sterling stablecoin issuers. For everyone else, the UK's regulatory picture is still incomplete and being actively written by the FCA.

### Verdict for Small Token-Issuing Companies

For a small company wanting to **issue tokens and access the European market**, **MiCA is the better option today**, despite being more complex. Three reasons:

1. **UK has no finished framework for general token issuance.** Operating there means legal ambiguity — not freedom. That ambiguity deters institutional partners, major exchanges, and serious investors.
2. **A UK license covers the UK. A MiCA license covers 27 countries.** For any project with European distribution ambitions, the math is straightforward.
3. **MiCA compliance is becoming a market standard.** Exchanges and custodians are increasingly requiring MiCA-compliant documentation. Being registered under MiCA opens doors; operating in UK regulatory limbo closes them.

**The one scenario where UK wins today:** you are specifically issuing a **sterling-denominated stablecoin** targeting UK retail and want to avoid EU reserve requirements for EUR stablecoins. That's a real edge case with a real business model — but it is not a general template for crypto startups.

## Part II — What the BoE Actually Changed

### The Old Proposal: Hard Caps That Killed the Product

The BoE's original 2023 consultation paper proposed hard limits on stablecoin holdings:
- **£20,000** per retail user
- **£10,000,000** per business

These caps were widely criticized as unworkable. A £20K retail limit makes sterling stablecoins useless for any meaningful settlement or treasury function. A £10M business cap would block institutional adoption entirely — a large company running payroll or supplier payments in stablecoin would hit this ceiling immediately.

The caps would have also created a structural disadvantage versus USD stablecoins (USDC, USDT), which face no such limits in most jurisdictions and already dominate institutional crypto flow.

### The New Rule: Aggregate Systemic Threshold

Instead of per-user caps, the BoE has replaced them with a **£40 billion aggregate issuer threshold**. Below this level, issuers operate under a lighter regulatory regime. Above it — or when approaching it — they enter enhanced systemic oversight, including closer coordination with the Bank, stress testing, and potential operational restrictions.

This is a more sensible design: it targets the actual systemic risk (a large stablecoin becoming too interconnected to fail) without destroying product utility for everyday use cases.

### Reserve Requirements

The new framework mandates that reserve assets for sterling stablecoins must be held in:
- **60% UK government gilts** (short-duration)
- **40% unremunerated accounts at the Bank of England**

The gilt requirement ensures the stablecoin ecosystem supports UK sovereign debt markets. The unremunerated BoE account requirement is a deliberate friction mechanism — issuers earn no yield on that 40% slice, which makes sterling stablecoins structurally less profitable than USD equivalents holding T-bills at positive yield.

This is intentional: the BoE does not want to create a product that competes directly with bank deposits or monetizes BoE balance sheet access at scale.

## Part III — MiCA's Architecture in Brief

For context, here is what the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation covers, fully in force as of July 1, 2026:

**Three token categories:**

1. **Asset-Referenced Tokens (ART)** — stablecoins pegged to a basket of currencies, commodities, or other assets. Require authorization from a national competent authority. Significant reserve and governance requirements.

2. **E-Money Tokens (EMT)** — stablecoins pegged 1:1 to a single fiat currency (e.g., EURC). Must be issued by a licensed e-money institution or bank. Subject to EU EMD2 requirements plus MiCA-specific rules.

3. **Other Crypto-Assets** — utility tokens, governance tokens, project tokens. Require a whitepaper filing and compliance with consumer protection rules, but licensing requirements are lighter than ART/EMT.

**The passport mechanism** is MiCA's most commercially important feature. An authorization granted in any EU member state — Cyprus, Malta, Lithuania, France, Germany — creates automatic passporting rights across all 27 states. A project licensed in Vilnius can legally offer services in Warsaw, Paris, and Amsterdam without re-applying.

This is structurally analogous to what the EU single financial passport did for traditional finance, and it represents genuine regulatory infrastructure value that the UK cannot currently offer.

## Part IV — Timeline and What Comes Next

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| July 1, 2026 | MiCA fully in force across EU |
| 2026 H2 | FCA expected to finalize UK stablecoin authorization rules |
| 2027 (est.) | FCA expected to publish broader crypto asset framework |
| TBD | UK utility token / general crypto asset rules — no confirmed date |

The UK is running approximately 12–18 months behind the EU on regulatory completeness. That gap is not necessarily permanent — the FCA has signaled it wants a pragmatic, principles-based framework rather than MiCA's prescriptive structure. Whether that turns into a competitive advantage (simpler, faster approvals) or a liability (unclear rules, inconsistent enforcement) remains to be seen.

For 2026, the practical reality is that **EU clarity beats UK optionality** for most serious crypto projects.

## Summary

The BoE's decision to drop holding caps is good news for sterling stablecoin issuers, removing a structural barrier that would have made the product commercially unviable. The systemic threshold model is more proportionate and aligns with how other systemic risk frameworks operate in finance.

But this development does not make the UK a broadly freer crypto jurisdiction than the EU. For the vast majority of crypto and token projects — utility tokens, governance tokens, DeFi protocols, non-sterling stablecoins — the relevant comparison is between UK regulatory ambiguity and MiCA's passport-backed clarity. In that comparison, MiCA wins on commercial utility in 2026.

The headline "UK eases crypto rules" is accurate but incomplete. The more useful framing: the UK has made a specific product (systemic sterling stablecoin) more viable. Everything else in the UK crypto landscape is still being figured out.
